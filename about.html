About This Project

When Alice stepped through the looking glass in 1871, there were no telephones, light bulbs or phonographs.

And yet, a mere 150 years later we carry our own looking glasses in our pocket that show us just about anything we'd like to see.

The idea of the tiny knitted QR code was inspired by Alice's first adventure.

As I worked through this project, I though of how Alice changed size and decided to pretend that my tools were not too small, it was simply I who was too large.

I built this project to serve as a window to updated calming views. You may see snow, rain, hikers or animals.

Take a deep breath and enjoy a window, shared.

How the Art Piece was Created

I had seen projects of people knitting QR codes into regularly sized knitting projects and knew it was possible.

Admittedly, I had no idea if this would work out.

I started by using quilting pins and standard polyester sewing thread, just to see if I could even manage.

There were many failed attempts at casting on the first evening, but I eventually got a swatch the size of my pinky nail.

I changed colors and pins often trying to figure out what worked best.

OK, so I could knit small. But could I do it cleanly enough to get it to scan? The answer was NO, not initially.

I tried to set myself up for the best success at the simplest QR code.

I created a project in GitHub and did some planning before generating the bit.ly link and accompanying QR code.

My First attempt was flat fair isle with a contrasting color border. My phone picked it up as text but would not scan or translate.

My Second attempt was flat fair isle with more border stitches. This one wouldn't scan at all.

For my third attempt, I tried double-knitting, but the contrasting color showed through and it wouldn't scan at all.

For my final, successful attempt, I created small double pointed needles by cutting the heads off of my pins and sanding them down

The final, successful attempt has a gauge of about 32 stitches x 32 rows per inch and took me about 4 hours to knit.

I was seriously considering pivoting my project because I hadn't felt like I'd made progress since my first attempt.

And then it scanned.

From here, all that was left was to choose some of my favorite views to share and build out the rest of the project.

How the Web Site was Created

I created a GitHub action to fetch updated images from a variety of National Park Services and US Geological Service static webcams once an hour.

Most of the cameras are in the continental US because I did not want viewers of this piece to be greeted with nighttime views.

The website itself just grabs one of the random images with a brief description. I tried to select views that would change based on weather or time of day.

As I've worked on this project I've seen animals, hikers, and show.

Happy Viewing!
